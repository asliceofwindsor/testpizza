The Creation of Windsor Pizza

Prior to World War II Windosr had various Italian restaurants, clubs, and businesses. Some restaurants that were opened even served Pizza. However, it was not until after World War II when Italian immigration to Windsor rapidly increased. With the new wave of immigrants, a new industry was born.

The Second Wave of Italian Immigration​

During World War II, concerns about fascism led to a halt in Italian immigration to Canada and the United States. Following World War II, economic opportunities encouraged immigration to Canada. Nearly 75% of Windsor's Italian population immigrated between 1940 and 1970.

Erie Street quickly became an expanding neighbourhood for Italian immigrants with numerous restaurants, bars, bakeries, and grocery stores. Erie Street was a cultural hub for Italian migrants and attracted non-Italians to its businesses and shops. As well, Erie Street became a place for Italian Americans to get a taste of home when their Little Italy declined. Many of these families created and ran their own businesses that hold legacies in Windsor today.

Windsor-style pizza emerged during this era. What the Windsor pizza industry became was not just about the ingredients themselves, but also about a distinct style, the specific sourcing of those ingredients, established business practices, family enviorments, and a loyal customer base.

The Creation of Windsor Style Pizza- Volcano Pizzeria

In 1957, Eugenio Manza and Frank Gualterri opened Volcanos Pizzeria, which is credited as the originator of Windsor Style Pizza. Eugenio (Gino) grew up in Calabria, working in the fields in Italy, learning to work hard from a young age. At 19, Gino moved to Manitoba with no money to work for CP Railroad. After 3 years, he moved to Windsor to be closer to his brother-in-law, Stan Cundari, a successful businessman who owned a pizza company in Detroit. Gino saw how popular Pizza was in Detroit and wanted to bring something new to Windsor. Gino worked hard to find the right equipment and a bank willing to lend him a loan. Needing help, Gino chose to work with his cousin, Frank Gualterri, borrowed money from family, and found a supplier who gave him 1 year to pay for the equipment.

The key to Volcanos' successful start was what became common among future Windsor pizza companies: hard work, a family environment, a loyal customer base, and a signature pizza style. Gino worked long hours to start his business because he was concerned about its opening amid a recession and uncertainty about customer response. Gino also realized that for his business to be successful, he needed strong employees to run his pizzeria. He chose people who were honest, friendly, and dependable. In 1957, Volcanos opened and was instantly successful, even without formal advertising, as he could not afford it. Customers quickly lined up along the street, and word of the pizza spread. The company quickly expanded from 60 seats to 400 seats and had a strong family environment for customers and employees. Employees would often have staff parties while customers ordered the pizza. When asked what he enjoyed about Volcanos, he stated, “I liked interacting with customers and was proud to see the young boys and girls I hired with no job experience later graduate and move on to become teachers, doctors, and lawyers.” Most importantly, Volcanos Pizzeria was the first to shred pepperoni, add cornmeal to its crust, and use canned mushrooms.

Many of Windsor's pizza makers got their start at Volcanos, such as Frank Tedesco, owner of Franco’s. Although Volcanos closed in 1986, Gino's legacy remains deeply rooted in the Windsor pizza industry. Volcanos set a standard for Windsor Pizza business practices, starting with hard work and creating a family environment. Many Windsor pizzerias today have deep connections with their staff, which leads to a strong working team. In addition, Volcanos valued its customers, valued interactions, and relied on word of mouth to keep the business alive. Finally, Volcanos set the standard for how the pizza was made, which modern Windsor pizzerias follow.

Early Success of Windsor Pizzerias

Arcata Pizza, Capri, Armando's, Francos, Naples, and Antonino’s are long-standing, widespread pizza chains in Windsor, all with roots dating back to the 1950s and 1960s. Many of the Windsor pizzerias today trace back to Tony Ciaravino, who established the first pizzeria in South Windsor, Capri, on Dougall Rd. in 1959. Capri Pizzeria was a popular hangout for celebrities who performed at the Elmwood Hotel. Tony then went on to own Arcata in the sixties, where the owner of Antonino’s Pizza (created in honour of Tony) and Tony’s son, Joe Ciaravino, grew up. Tony held a firm foundation in three of the largest Windsor pizza companies: Windsor, Capri, Arcata, and Antonino’s.

Notoriety of Windsor-Style Pizza

From Volcanos to the many notable pizzerias of the 1950s to 60s, these pizzerias established a clear identity shared by the people of Windsor and across the border in Detroit. For example, a 1987 Detroit Free Press newspaper explains how Madonna ordered an authentic Windsor Pizza after a show in Detroit. Additionally, in a 1993 Detroit Free Press article, a former Windsor resident wrote to find a Windsor-style pizza in Detroit, like Franco's Pizza, with zesty sauce and generous toppings, not floppy like Detroit Pizza.

These early-established Windsor pizzerias were founded by Italian families, starting with Volcano Pizzeria, which inspired other family-owned Windsor-style pizzerias. As noted in the newspaper articles, Windsor Pizza was influential among Windsor citizens and carried its notoriety across the border, helping establish Windsor as a distinct identity following World War II. Starting with Volcanos, a culture of hard work, family environments, strong customer relationships, quality service, and distinct ingredients made the industry successful.
